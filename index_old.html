**(1) Introduction and Primary Argument**

This comment challenges the proposed NOx emission limits for the Keetac Grate Kiln indurating furnace, specifically addressing the limit for mixed fuel scenarios presented on page 17239, paragraph (b)(1)(i)(A)(2), (C)(1), and (C)(3): "An emission limit of 2.0 lbs NOx/MMBtu, based on a 720-hr rolling average, shall apply . . . when burning any fuel or combination of fuels other than exclusively natural gas . . . EPA is also proposing to allow Keetac, within a period of 52 months from the effective date of the final rule, the option to seek a potential adjustment of the cofiring emission limit, not to exceed 2.5 lbs NOx/MMBtu . . . based on collection of CEMS data after installation of the NOx reduction technology." The arbitrary 2.0 lbs NOx/MMBtu limit, along with the provision allowing for a post-hoc adjustment up to 2.5 lbs NOx/MMBtu, are unsupported by the preamble. The EPA's own justification cites a lack of validation for cofiring scenarios due to the facility not utilizing mixed fuels after 2013 (p. 17237). Establishing an enforceable limit based on unvalidated modeling is premature and procedurally flawed. The language should be revised to remove the initial 2.0 lbs NOx/MMBtu limit entirely, replacing it with a requirement for robust testing and modeling *prior* to establishing any limit for mixed-fuel scenarios. Only after this crucial data is gathered and analyzed can a reasonable and enforceable BART determination be made. This data-driven approach would align with the EPA’s stated concern for validating modeling results with operational CEMS data and ensure the chosen limit does not negatively impact Keetac's operations while still achieving meaningful emission reductions.

**(2) Rationale for Proposed Changes**

The proposed structure for setting the mixed-fuel limit inverts the established BART determination process outlined in the EPA's own BART Guidelines (40 CFR part 51, appendix Y). These guidelines emphasize a thorough analysis of available control technologies and their feasibility *before* setting emission limits. By establishing a preliminary limit based on unvalidated modeling and allowing for later adjustment, the EPA risks implementing a standard that is either overly stringent, potentially impacting plant operations and pellet quality, or insufficient for achieving necessary emission reductions. The EPA's reliance on unvalidated modeling for the mixed fuel limit contrasts sharply with the careful analysis recommended in the BART Guidelines.

(1) Introduction and Primary Argument

This comment addresses the proposed rule's reliance on compliance margins as the primary justification for approving alternative monitoring requirements for NOx emissions from large non-EGUs in Indiana, as presented on page 16661, paragraphs 40-42. Specifically, the statement "Given the substantial compliance margin in this instance, EPA believes that the data monitored and reported under Indiana's alternative monitoring requirements would be sufficient to determine whether the State's non-EGUs are in compliance with their collective emissions cap" is challengeable. This reliance on past performance and substantial compliance margins is insufficient to guarantee the accuracy and reliability of future emissions data under the proposed alternative monitoring methods. The rule should be revised to include more stringent criteria for evaluating the effectiveness of alternative monitoring methods, rather than relying solely on historical compliance margins. This should include a detailed analysis comparing the precision and accuracy of the alternative methods with the existing 40 CFR Part 75 requirements, ensuring that any approved alternative provides comparable data quality.


(2) Rationale for Change

The EPA's assertion that substantial compliance margins justify less rigorous monitoring methodologies is fundamentally flawed. Compliance margins are retrospective metrics and do not predict future emissions behavior or guarantee the ongoing accuracy of less precise measurement techniques. Changes in fuel composition, operational parameters, or maintenance practices can significantly impact NOx emissions, potentially leading to underestimation and non-compliance even with historically large margins. Furthermore, relying solely on compliance margins disincentivizes proactive emissions reductions and technological advancements in monitoring. The proposed rule should require a robust comparison of the proposed alternative monitoring methods to the existing 40 CFR Part 75 standards, using established performance metrics such as bias, precision, and accuracy. This analysis should demonstrate that any approved alternative method achieves comparable data quality and ensures reliable emissions tracking. The EPA should consider incorporating performance specifications similar to those outlined in 40 CFR Part 60 for continuous emission monitoring systems, to guarantee the reliability and accuracy of the chosen alternative methods. Studies have highlighted the importance of accurate and reliable emissions data for effective air quality management.
