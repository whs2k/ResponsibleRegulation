The NOX SIP Call was specifically designed to reduce interstate transport of ozone by requiring states to implement controls on NOX emissions from significant sources, including large non-EGUs.

The continued absence of these enforceable limits for large non-EGUs means that a category of significant pollution sources has been operating without the federally mandated emission constraints for over a decade. PM2.5 exposure is linked to cardiovascular diseases, respiratory illnesses, and premature mortality.

This comment addresses the Environmental Protection Agency's (EPA) proposed rule [EPA-R05-OAR-2024-0216; FRL-12599-01-R5], specifically the proposed modifications to Nitrogen Oxide (NOx) emission limits for the indurating furnace at United States Steel's (U.S. Steel's) Keetac taconite facility. * "Modeling of the Keetac indurating furnace with a low-NO<INF>X</INF> main burner in conjunction with low-NO<INF>X</INF> preheat burners indicated that a limit of **3.4 lbs NO<INF>X</INF>/MMBtu represents BART** for Keetac when firing exclusively natural gas." (Page 17236)

These proposed limits are significantly weaker than the limits established in the Original 2013 FIP Rule (1.2 lbs NOx/MMBtu for natural gas and 1.5 lbs NOx/MMBtu for other fuels). While the EPA provides justification based on Keetac-specific modeling and studies provided by U.S. Steel, the proposed limits may not represent the "best" available retrofit technology as mandated by the Clean Air Act (CAA). We propose that the EPA re-evaluate the feasibility of more stringent NOx controls, potentially closer to the levels demonstrated as achievable with Low-NOx Burner (LNB) technology in other similar industrial applications, or provide a more robust, independent analysis justifying why such significant upward revisions from the 2013 FIP are necessary and still constitute BART. The proposed adjustment mechanism for the cofiring limit, particularly allowing an increase up to 2.5 lbs NO<INF>X</INF>/MMBtu, should be removed, or at a minimum, the threshold for such an adjustment should be more rigorously defined and justified as still meeting BART requirements, rather than being based primarily on initial operational data post-installation of a technology that itself is leading to a weaker standard.

**(2) Rationale for Change or Removal:**

The proposed NOx emission limits of 3.4 lbs/MMBtu (natural gas) and 2.0 lbs/MMBtu (cofiring, potentially adjustable to 2.5 lbs/MMBtu) represent a substantial relaxation from the original 2013 FIP limits. The justification for this change relies heavily on modeling and engineering studies, many of which appear to be commissioned or provided by the operator, U.S. Steel (as referenced on page 17236, footnotes 9-17). While facility-specific factors are important in BART determinations, the CAA requires the "best" available retrofit technology, considering cost, energy, and non-air quality environmental impacts (40 CFR §51.301; Appendix Y to Part 51—Guidelines for BART Determinations). The current proposal suggests an anticipated NOx reduction of only 40-45% (page 17237, column 1, and footnote 23), significantly less than the 70% reduction anticipated in the 2013 FIP (page 17237, column 1). This raises concerns about whether the full spectrum of LNB designs and operational optimizations, or combinations with other technologies, has been exhaustively and independently evaluated by the EPA to ensure the proposed limits truly reflect "best" available performance. Modern LNB technology, when properly designed and optimized, can often achieve NOx reductions significantly greater than 40-45% in various industrial applications. For instance, advancements in burner design and staging techniques continue to push achievable NOx emission levels lower (Garg, A. (2019). Chapter 6: Low-NOx Combustion. In C.E. Baukal Jr. (Ed.), *The John Zink Hamworthy Combustion Handbook: Volume 1 - Fundamentals* (2nd ed.). CRC Press. Available: `https://www.taylorfrancis.com/books/edit/10.1201/9780429283104/john-zink-hamworthy-combustion-handbook-charles-baukal-jr`). Furthermore, the EPA’s own BART guidelines emphasize a thorough five-factor analysis and consideration of available control options (`https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-40/chapter-I/subchapter-C/part-51/appendix-Appendix%20Y%20to%20Part%2051`). The agency should provide a more detailed public record of its independent assessment of why alternative LNB configurations or other synergistic control strategies (even if SNCR was deemed unsuitable, a detailed re-evaluation of its potential optimized application or other post-combustion controls in conjunction with LNBs) cannot achieve lower emission rates at Keetac without imposing unreasonable costs or operational detriments that would disqualify them under the BART analysis. The provision to allow an upward adjustment of the cofiring limit to 2.5 lbs/MMBtu based on initial operational data appears to preemptively concede that even the relaxed 2.0 lbs/MMBtu limit may not be met, which undermines the stringency expected from a BART determination. Instead, the BART limit should be set based on a robust a priori assessment of what is achievable, with compliance expected.

**(1) Introduction and Primary Argument:**

This comment addresses the Environmental Protection Agency's (EPA) proposed rule, "Air Plan Approval; Indiana; Indiana NOX Emissions Monitoring," published in the Federal Register, Volume 90, Number 75, on Monday, April 21, 2025, specifically pages 16658-16663. We challenge the approval of Indiana rule 326 IAC 10-2-8.5, as described on page 16660 of the Federal Register, which introduces alternative NOX monitoring, record keeping, and reporting requirements for large non-Electric Generating Units (non-EGUs).

Specifically, we are concerned with the provisions under **326 IAC 10-2-8.5(c)(1)(D)**, as described on page 16660: "monitoring of heat input and fuel use combined with an approved emission factors for NOX emissions rates to compute NOX mass emissions..." While we appreciate the EPA's and Indiana's intent to provide flexibility consistent with the March 8, 2019 (84 FR 8422) amendments to the NOX SIP Call regulations, the reliance on "approved emission factors" for calculating NOX mass emissions for these large non-EGUs presents a significant risk of underestimating actual emissions, thereby potentially undermining the integrity of the NOX SIP Call objectives and the protection of public health.

We propose that **326 IAC 10-2-8.5(c)(1)(D)** be modified to require that any use of emission factors be restricted to very small sources or for very limited periods during CEMS malfunction, and that for large non-EGUs, the primary alternative methods should prioritize direct measurement or more robust parametric monitoring correlated with frequent testing. Alternatively, if emission factors are permitted for large non-EGUs, their application must be accompanied by a requirement for more frequent (e.g., annual or biennial) source-specific stack testing to validate and update these factors, rather than the proposed "at least every five years" as stated in 326 IAC 10-2-8.5(e). The current language, even with a five-year stack test requirement, does not sufficiently ensure the accuracy needed for compliance with a collective emissions cap, especially if the "substantial compliance margin" cited by EPA (page 16661) diminishes over time or for individual units operating closer to their potential emissions.

**(2) Rationale for Change or Removal:**

The rationale for our concern and proposed modifications to 326 IAC 10-2-8.5(c)(1)(D) and the associated paragraph (e) stems from the inherent uncertainties and potential for inaccuracies associated with using generic or infrequently updated emission factors for large non-EGUs. The NOX SIP Call aims to reduce transported NOX emissions, a precursor to ozone, which has significant public health impacts. Accurate emissions accounting is paramount to the success of this program.

1. **Limitations of Emission Factors:** Standard emission factors, such as those from EPA's AP-42, are generally averages of data from various sources and may not accurately represent the specific operational characteristics, fuel variability, or control equipment performance of an individual unit. The EPA itself notes that emission factors have varying degrees of reliability, often rated from A (excellent) to E (poor). Relying on such factors, especially for large emitters, can lead to significant discrepancies between reported and actual emissions.

2. **Superiority of Direct Measurement:** Continuous Emissions Monitoring Systems (CEMS) as required by 40 CFR Part 75, or even well-calibrated Part 60 CEMS, provide a much more accurate and continuous record of emissions. While the proposed rule allows for alternatives to Part 75, the alternative involving emission factors (326 IAC 10-2-8.5(c)(1)(D)) represents the lowest tier of accuracy among the options. The EPA’s own framework for emissions trading programs and compliance has historically prioritized CEMS due to their accuracy and ability to capture real-time operational variability.
* **Reference:** U.S. Environmental Protection Agency. "Part 75 Emissions Monitoring, Reporting and Recordkeeping." This page details the stringent requirements of Part 75, emphasizing quality assurance and accuracy for programs like the Acid Rain Program and CSAPR, which rely on high-quality data. 3. **Impact of Infrequent Stack Testing:** While 326 IAC 10-2-8.5(e) requires stack tests at least every five years for units using emission factors, this frequency is insufficient to ensure ongoing accuracy for large non-EGUs. Combustion conditions, fuel quality, and control device efficiency can change significantly over a five-year period, leading to emission factors that no longer reflect actual operations. This can result in a cumulative underestimation (or, less likely, overestimation) of emissions over several ozone seasons. A study by the Northeast States for Coordinated Air Use Management (NESCAUM) has highlighted that even for smaller sources, actual emissions can deviate significantly from factor-based estimates if not regularly verified.

This not only undermines the NOX SIP Call but also fails to provide the robust data necessary for future air quality planning and public health protection.

Therefore, we urge the EPA to require modifications to Indiana's proposed 326 IAC 10-2-8.5 to ensure that any alternative monitoring methods, especially those relying on emission factors for large non-EGUs, incorporate more stringent validation and accuracy requirements to uphold the environmental and public health objectives of the Clean Air Act.
